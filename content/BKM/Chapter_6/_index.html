{{% notice warning %}} Nongovernmental investors cannot borrow at the risk-free rate. Therefore, in the borrowing range of the CAL, the risk-free \(r_f\) rate becomes the borrowing rate \(r_B > r_f\) and as a result the CAL will be kinked. {{% /notice %}} ## 6.5 Risk Tolerance and Asset Allocation

It is natural to ask what allocation(s) maximize utility.

\[ \begin{align} \mathop{argmax}_y (U) &= E(r_C) - \frac{1}{2} A \sigma^2_C \\ &= r_f + y[E(r_P) - r_f] - \frac{1}{2} A y^2 \sigma^2_P \end{align}\]

We proceed by taking the derivative of both sides with respect to \(y\).

\[ \begin{align} 0 &= E(r_P) - r_f - Ay^*\sigma^2_P \\ \implies y^* &= \frac{E(r_P) - r_f}{A\sigma^2_P} \end{align} \]

Note that the risky allocation percentage is:

  • directly proportional to the risk premium of the risky asset
  • inversely proportional to the risk aversion of the investor
  • inversely proportional to the variance of the risky asset

Example

Suppose the risk aversion of an investor is 4, the risk-free rate is 7%, the expected return of the risky asset is 15% with standard deviation 22%. What is the optimal percent investment in the risky-asset for this investor?

\[y^* = \frac{0.15 - 0.07}{4 * 0.22^2} = 41\% \]

With this optimal allocation, the return on the complete portfolio will be $E(r_C) = 0.07 + 0.41 (0.15 - 0.07) = 10.28% $ The standard deviation will be $_C = 0.41 * 0.22 = 9.02% $.
